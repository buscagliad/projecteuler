Let's just do an example. Let's find the continued fraction for 55. 52.2352.23 or something, and a0a0 is the integer part of this, which is 2.

Then we subtract a0a0 from 55 and take the reciprocal. That is, we calculate 152152. If you're using a calculator, this comes out to 4.23 or so. Then a1a1 is the integer part of this, which is 4. So:

5=2+14+15=2+14+1

Where we haven't figured out the part yet. To get that, we take our 4.234.23, subtract a1a1, and take the reciprocal; that is, we calculate 14.2344.2314.2344.23. This is just the same as we had before, so a2a2 is 4 again, and continuing in the same way, a3=a4==4a3=a4==4:

5=2+14+14+14+15=2+14+14+14+1

This procedure will work for any number whatever, but for 55 we can use a little algebraic cleverness to see that the fours really do repeat. When we get to the 152152 stage, we apply algebra to convert this to 1525+25+2=5+21525+25+2=5+2. So we could say that:

52+5=2+12+5=4+12+5.5=2+12+52+5=4+12+5.

If we substitute the right-hand side of the last equation expression into itself in place of 2+52+5, we get:

2+5=4+14+12+5=4+14+14+12+5=4+14+14+14+12+5=2+5=4+14+12+5=4+14+14+12+5=4+14+14+14+12+5=

and it's evident that the fours will repeat forever.

a0a0 is the largest integer that is smaller than or equal to nn. Or put another way, you want a20a02 to be smaller than or equal to nn, and (a0+1)2(a0+1)2 to be bigger than nn.

If you really have no idea what integer to use, then you find it by guessing an integer gg. Then you calculate g2g2. If g2g2 is bigger than nn, your guess gg was too big, and you try a smaller guess. If g2g2 is much smaller than nn, your guess gg was too small, and you try a bigger guess. You keep doing this until you find a guess gg where g2ng2n and (g+1)2>n(g+1)2>n, and then a0=ga0=g.

For the record, the continued fraction expansion of a real number x0=xx0=x is found by computing an=xnan=xn and then xn+1=1/(xnan)xn+1=1/(xnan).

For specific examples and intuition, see the other answers to this post. Here I describe a simple procedure for computing the expansion of NN that involves only integer arithmetic.

The motivation is that we can always write xn=(rn+N)/snxn=(rn+N)/sn for integers rnrn and snsn.

We compute the sequences anan, rnrn and snsn inductively:

First, compute a0=Na0=N. This is just the largest integer a0a0 with a20Na02N. Set r0=0r0=0 and s0=1s0=1. Then for each nn, define

rn+1=ansnrnrn+1=ansnrn
sn+1=(Nr2n+1)/sn.sn+1=(Nrn+12)/sn.
